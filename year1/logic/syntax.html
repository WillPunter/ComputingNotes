Propositional Logic Syntax

Definitions

  • Logic - a logic is a system of reasoning. A logic is composed of:
    • Syntax - the formal language of the logic (comparable to a programming language).
    • Semantics - the meanings associated with the symbols in the syntax.
    • Proof theory - a syntactic way of identifying valid statements in the language (i.e. evaluating a string of symbols to be true or false).
  • Components of propositional logic syntax:
    • Propositional atoms - true or false values
    • Boolean connectives - not (\(¬\)), and (\(\wedge\)), or (\(\vee\)), if-then (\(\rightarrow\)), if-and-only-if (\(\leftrightarrow\)), true (\(\top\), also called top), false (\(\bot\), also called bottom)
    • Punctuation - we use brackets to disambiguate formulas, e.g. \((p \wedge q) \vee r\) is different to \(p \wedge (q \vee r)\)
  • Propositional formula - a propositional formula is a string of symbols made from propositional formulas. Formally, a propositional formula is defined as any of the following:
    • A propositional atom
    • \(\top\) and \(\bot\)
    • If \(\phi\) is a formula then (\(¬\phi\)) is as well (the brackets are important)
    • If \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) are formulas then (\(\phi \wedge \psi\)), (\(\phi \vee \psi\)), (\(\phi \rightarrow \psi\)) and (\(\phi \leftrightarrow \psi\)) are formulas
    • Nothing else is a formula. Note that the first two bullet points may be referred to as "atomic" formulas.
  • Binding conventions - the binding conventions determine how formulas without brackets should be interpretted. From strongest to weakest, the binding conventions for the Boolean connectives are: \(¬, \wedge, \vee, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow\)
    • E.g. \(p \vee q \rightarrow ¬r \wedge s\) should be interpretted as \((p \vee q) \rightarrow ((¬r) \wedge s)\)
    • Note that repeated connectives are ambiguous - for some boolean connectives it doesn't matter (e.g. \(p \wedge q \wedge r \equiv p \wedge (q \wedge r) \equiv (p \wedge q) \wedge r\)) but for others it does - you should never write repeated boolean connectives in your formulas
  • Formation tree - every logical formula has a corresponding formation tree. Formation trees are formed by splitting down a formula into subformulas via its weakest boolean connective.

  • Atomic formula - a formua of the form \(\top, \bot\) or \(p\), where \(p\) is a propositional atom.
  • Negated formula - a formula of the form \(¬\phi\) where \(\phi\) is a propositional formula.
  • Conjunction - a formula of the form \(\phi \wedge \psi\).
  • Disjunction - a formula of the form \(\phi \vee \psi\).
  • Implication - a formula of the form \(\phi \rightarrow \psi\).
  • Bidirectional implication - a formula of the form \(\phi \leftrightarrow \psi\).
  • Literal - a formula that is either atomic or negated atomic (i.e. made up of \(\top, \bot\) or propositional atom \(p\), or the negation of such a formula).
  • Clause - a formula that is a disjunction of one or more literals.
